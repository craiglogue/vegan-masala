Lentils and dal are at the heart of many Indian meals. They are affordable, high in protein, and incredibly versatile.

If you are new to Indian cooking, understanding a few common types of dal will make it much easier to choose the right one for each dish.

## The Main Types of Dal

These are some of the most common lentils and split pulses used in Indian cooking.

[CARD: Red lentils (masoor dal) | /images/guides/placeholder.jpg | Quick-cooking lentils that break down easily and make soft, comforting dal.]
[CARD: Moong dal | /images/guides/placeholder.jpg | Light and easy to digest, often used in simple everyday recipes.]
[CARD: Urad dal | /images/guides/placeholder.jpg | Richer and darker, often used in heartier dishes such as dal makhani.]
[CARD: Chana dal | /images/guides/placeholder.jpg | Split chickpeas with a firmer texture and slightly nutty flavour.]
[CARD: Toor dal | /images/guides/placeholder.jpg | Common in many Indian homes, especially in South Indian and Gujarati cooking.]
[CARD: Whole lentils | /images/guides/placeholder.jpg | Useful when you want more texture and body in a dish.]

## Which Dal to Use

Different dals create different textures.

### For soft, quick dal

- red lentils
- moong dal

### For richer, heartier dishes

- urad dal
- chana dal

### For everyday comfort food

- toor dal
- red lentils

## Cooking Tips

- Rinse dal before cooking.
- Some dals cook quickly, while others need longer simmering.
- Add more water when reheating because dal thickens as it sits.

## Final Thoughts

You do not need to stock every type of dal at once. Starting with red lentils, moong dal, and one heartier option will already give you a lot of flexibility in vegan Indian cooking.
